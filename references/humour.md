# Humour — how to build a humour profile

This is a **template**. For each new client, decide *whether* the brand uses
humour and, if so, *how*. Read the finished profile before writing any
long-form content.

Humour is one of the strongest signals that a human wrote the copy. But it
must fit the niche. A plumber can be funny on every page. A medical
practice, a law firm or a funeral service should be warm and human without
jokes. **Decide the humour level first; then calibrate.**

---

## Step 1 — decide the humour level

| Level | Fits | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| **Funny** | trades, hospitality, fitness, consumer services, agencies | Jokes are mandatory; every long page lands several |
| **Light** | retail, local services, B2B with personality | Occasional warmth and a wink; no set-piece jokes |
| **Dry / none** | medical, legal, finance, funeral, high-luxury | No jokes. Warmth comes from plain speech and honesty |

If the level is "dry / none", the rest of this file does not apply — keep
copy precise, human and unpretentious, and let
[`stories.md`](stories.md) and [`opinions.md`](opinions.md) carry the
personality instead.

---

## Step 2 — if the brand is funny, calibrate

### The north-star
Pin the humour to a *specific person* (see [`voice.md`](voice.md)) — their
age, their references, their relationship to the trade. Humour with a
named source lands; free-floating "brand humour" reads as AI.

### Five rules
1. **Topical, not random.** Every joke is about the subject at hand —
   a pun on the actual problem, not a generic gag.
2. **Punch at yourself, the industry, or physics — never at the reader.**
   The reader called because something broke. Self-deprecation is free real
   estate. Roasting the customer is not.
3. **Parenthetical asides** are the cheapest humour that works — little
   winks that remind the reader a person wrote this.
4. **References that age well.** Long-lived culture, not this month's
   meme. A dated reference dates the whole page.
5. **The sign-off lands.** The last line should make the reader smile —
   not "We're here to help 24/7".

### Anti-patterns
- Exclamation marks — jokes land better deadpan; the joke is the
  punctuation.
- Emojis.
- Laughing at the customer.
- Forced quirk — if a joke isn't funny it's just weird; cut it.
- The same joke twice in one page.
- More than one meta joke about the writing itself.

### Frequency
Roughly **one humour moment every 300–500 words** — a joke, a self-aware
aside, or a culture drop. Not every paragraph (exhausting), but often
enough that the reader's face moves. A ~1,500-word post: 3–5 jokes, 2–3
asides, 1–2 parenthetical dips, 1 landing sign-off.

### Vibe check
Read it back in your head. It should sound like a real person in the trade
talking to a neighbour — not a brochure, not a LinkedIn post, not a
stand-up set, not an AI trying to be funny. Too dry → add. Too much →
dial back.

## Worked example — Plumbing Co (level: Funny)

> Marco is 52, Italian-Australian, a Prahran plumber since 2009. Dad-joke
> energy, owned and unapologetic. Every blog post is funny.
>
> *"My toilet has been running so long I'm thinking of signing it up for a
> marathon."* Topic stated, pun delivered, move on — that's the pattern.
> One pun on the post's actual subject, self-deprecation about his
> apprentice, a parenthetical aside, a sign-off that smiles. Deadpan. No
> exclamation marks, ever.
