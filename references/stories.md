# Stories — how to build a stories profile

This is a **template**. For each new client, collect a small set of real,
recurring anecdotes from their working life. Draw on them when a page needs
a human moment. **Never invent a story** — if none fits, write the page
without one.

A specific, true story does what no adjective can: it proves the business
is real and the person telling it has actually done the work.

---

## How to build it

Get 5–8 real anecdotes from the client. For each, capture:

- **The story** — what happened, in two or three sentences.
- **One concrete, checkable detail** — a year, a street, a price, a part,
  a name. The detail is what makes it land.
- **When to use it** — which topics or page types it suits.

Useful story types to ask the client for:

| Type | What it does |
|---|---|
| **Origin story** | Why the business exists — anchors trust and positioning |
| **The recurring problem** | The job the client sees over and over — explains a common topic with authority |
| **The honest-outcome story** | A time honesty cost a sale or won a loyal customer |
| **The fast-response story** | A real example of the service at its best |
| **The "what the last operator did" story** | A recurring thing customers report — sets up why this business is different |

---

## How to use stories in content

- **At most one story per page.** More than one feels like padding.
- **Don't recite verbatim.** Adapt the bones — the person, the timeframe,
  the one concrete detail — to fit the page.
- **Always include the one concrete detail.** Without it the story reads
  invented, which is worse than no story.
- **Anonymise customers** unless the client has explicit permission — "a
  customer in [suburb]", "a job last winter".
- **Never invent.** If no real story fits the topic, leave it out.

## Worked example — Plumbing Co

> One story per blog post, picked for topical fit.

- **Origin — the Windsor garage:** Marco left a franchise in 2009 because
  its training was scripts for upselling, not plumbing. Started Plumbing Co
  from a borrowed Windsor garage with one van. Use for: trust, why the
  business is flat-fee.
- **The fat-in-the-candle story:** nine of ten blocked Prahran kitchen
  sinks are fat — it goes down warm and sets like a candle a metre into the
  pipe. Use for: blocked sinks, why blockages recur.
- **The aerator job:** called out for "no pressure at all", Marco rinsed
  the tap aerator and fixed it in 90 seconds — still had to charge the
  $149 call-out. Use for: low pressure, DIY-first advice.
- **The 90-minute Sunday burst:** onsite in 40 minutes, water back on in
  90, charged exactly the phone quote. Use for: emergency response, no
  price surprises.
