# Voice — how to build a client voice profile

This is a **template**. For each new client, replace the worked example at
the bottom with that client's real voice. Read the finished profile before
writing any page of the site.

The voice profile answers: *who is writing, and how do they sound?*

---

## How to build it

Gather these from the client brief, the keyword CSV niche, and a short
conversation with the user. If a detail is unknown, ask once — don't invent
a personality the client wouldn't recognise.

### 1. Who is writing
A named person where possible — owner, founder, lead. Age, background, how
long in the trade, what they personally do day-to-day. Copy written by *a
person* beats copy written by *a brand*. If the client is genuinely
faceless, define a consistent house voice instead.

### 2. Sentence rhythm
- Sentence length — short and punchy, or measured and considered?
- One idea per sentence vs. layered clauses.
- **Always: start with the answer, then add context.** This is universal.
- Pacing of any humour (see [`humour.md`](humour.md)).

### 3. Words they use
The 8–12 phrases, contractions and bits of vocabulary that sound like this
specific person/brand. Trade slang, regionalisms, favourite numeric intros
("nine times out of ten"), how they refer to themselves and competitors.

### 4. Words they never use
Two lists:
- **Universal bans** (every client): "unlock", "leverage", "empower",
  "synergy", "seamless", "cutting-edge", "world-class", "best-in-class",
  "in today's fast-paced world", "we pride ourselves on", "contact us",
  "reach out", "utilize", exclamation marks, emojis.
- **Client-specific bans:** empty signals this brand in particular should
  avoid — e.g. "affordable", "quality service", "competitive pricing".

### 5. Formatting rules
- Headings are statements, not labels.
- "We" for the business, "I" when the named person speaks personally.
- Real numbers over adjectives — always.
- How prices are framed (flat, "from", all-in — never "starting at").

### 6. Tells that it's AI-written
The checklist to run before shipping anything. Delete any paragraph that
matches:
- Three-item lists where every item starts with an "-ing" verb.
- Paragraphs ending on a rhetorical flourish instead of a fact.
- "Whether you're X, Y, or Z…" intro sentences.
- Parallel sentence structures repeating across paragraphs.
- The word "comprehensive".
- Bullet lists where every bullet is a full sentence ending in a period.
- A closing sentence that restates the opening.

### 7. One-line summary
A single sentence the writer can hold in their head: "Write like ___."

## Worked example — Marco D'Agostino, Plumbing Co

> Use the *shape*, not the content. Every client gets their own.

**Who is writing.** Marco D'Agostino. 52. Owner-operator of Plumbing Co,
Prahran. Licensed plumber since 2004; started the business in 2009 out of a
borrowed Windsor garage. Still answers the phone himself. Writes like he's
explaining it to a neighbour at the pub, one beer in.

**Sentence rhythm.** Short sentences, mostly under 18 words. One idea each.
Answer first, context after. Dad-joke pacing: set up, land, walk away.

**Words he uses.** "reckon", "call-out", "mate" (asides only), "the other
guy", "nine times out of ten", "fair enough", "honest numbers", "rule of
thumb", "give us a call", "the van", "onsite".

**Words he never uses.** The universal bans, plus "affordable",
"competitive pricing", "quality service".

**Formatting.** Headings are statements ("Three steps. No surprises."). "I"
in blog posts, "we" for the business. Prices always "$149 flat" / "from
$149" — never "starting at".

**One-line summary.** Write like the neighbour whose ceiling just caved in
is talking to — one beer in, genuinely helpful, physically unable to resist
a bad pun.
